- **Roger Deakins** (visual consultant, fantasy work): Natural lighting motivation even in magical contexts, subtle color grading, atmospheric depth through haze/mist

## SHOT SIZE OPTIONS

Choose shot size based on narrative purpose and emotional intent. Each option references specific director/cinematographer techniques:

1. **ECU (Extreme Close-Up)** - *Focal Length: 85mm-135mm, f/1.4-f/2.0*
   - **Director Reference:** **Peter Jackson** (LOTR: The One Ring close-ups, Frodo's eye reflecting Gollum)
   - **Use for:** Intense emotion, magical artifact details, eye reflections showing wonder/fear, texture of aged prophecy scrolls
   - **Example:** Character's eye reflecting incoming dragon fire, hand gripping ancient sword hilt with glowing runes
   - **Technique:** Shallow depth isolates subject, background blur emphasizes magic/emotion

2. **CU (Close-Up)** - *Focal Length: 50mm-85mm, f/2.0-f/2.8*
   - **Director Reference:** **Guillermo del Toro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Ofelia's face discovering magic, intimate horror moments)
   - **Use for:** Emotional reactions to magic, character determination, intimate dialogue moments
   - **Example:** Warrior's face illuminated by magical glow, sweat and determination visible
   - **Technique:** Standard lens for natural perspective, emotional intimacy without distortion

3. **MCU (Medium Close-Up)** - *Focal Length: 35mm-50mm, f/2.8-f/4.0*
   - **Director Reference:** **Ang Lee** (Crouching Tiger: Dialogue scenes balancing character and environment)
   - **Use for:** Spell-casting gestures, weapon handling, character reactions with partial environment context
   - **Example:** Wizard's upper body as mystical energy swirls around raised hands
   - **Technique:** Shows hand gestures and facial expression together, ideal for spellcasting

4. **MS (Medium Shot)** - *Focal Length: 24mm-35mm, f/4.0-f/5.6*
   - **Director Reference:** **Zack Snyder** (300: Warrior combat poses, slow-motion heroic action)
   - **Use for:** Combat action, character interaction, showing character equipment/costume in detail
   - **Example:** Knight drawing sword, full armor visible, castle courtyard in background
   - **Technique:** Shows weapon handling and body language, perfect for action choreography

5. **MWS (Medium Wide Shot)** - *Focal Length: 24mm, f/5.6-f/8.0*
   - **Director Reference:** **Peter Jackson** (LOTR: Characters in hobbit holes, fellowship in environments)
   - **Use for:** Character within magical environment, group formations, environmental storytelling
   - **Example:** Hero standing before ancient temple entrance, full body visible with towering statues flanking
   - **Technique:** Balances character and setting, shows spatial relationships

6. **WS (Wide Shot)** - *Focal Length: 16mm-24mm, f/8.0-f/11*
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Andrew Lesnie** (LOTR: Fellowship walking across landscape, Rohan riders)
   - **Use for:** Establishing fantasy locations, battle formations, environmental scale, creature reveals
   - **Example:** Army marching across vast plains toward distant dark fortress, storm clouds gathering
   - **Technique:** Deep focus keeps foreground and background sharp, epic environmental context

7. **EWS (Extreme Wide Shot)** - *Focal Length: 14mm-16mm, f/11-f/16*
   - **Director Reference:** **Peter Jackson** (LOTR: Establishing shots of Rivendell, Minas Tirith, Mordor)
   - **Use for:** Epic landscape establishing shots, world-building, showing journey scope, final victory vistas
   - **Example:** Tiny fellowship crossing snow-covered mountain pass, peaks stretching endlessly
   - **Technique:** Ultra-wide anamorphic for maximum scope, characters small against vast world

## CAMERA ANGLE OPTIONS

Select angle based on emotional subtext and power dynamics. Each angle has specific director precedents:

1. **Eye Level** - Neutral, immersive, relatable character perspective
   - **Director Reference:** **Ang Lee** (Crouching Tiger: Natural POV for intimate character conversations)
   - **Effect:** Audience feels equal to character, immersive realism, relatable perspective

2. **Low Angle** - Heroic stature, divine power, intimidating presence, monumental architecture
   - **Director Reference:** **Peter Jackson** (LOTR: Aragorn heroic moments, Gandalf revealing power)
   - **Effect:** Character appears larger, powerful, commanding; makes architecture feel monumental

3. **High Angle** - Vulnerability, powerlessness, showing tactical battlefield overview, gods-eye-view
   - **Director Reference:** **Peter Jackson** (LOTR: Frodo alone in vast landscapes, hobbits in danger)
   - **Effect:** Character appears small, vulnerable, overwhelmed by environment

4. **Dutch Angle (Tilted)** - Magical chaos, reality distortion, unease, dark magic influence
   - **Director Reference:** **Guillermo del Toro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Pale Man scene, reality vs fantasy shifts)
   - **Effect:** 15-45Â° tilt creates unease, signals magic corrupting reality, psychological disturbance

5. **Bird's Eye View** - Strategic battle view, fate/destiny perspective, massive scale events
   - **Director Reference:** **Zack Snyder** (300: Battle of Thermopylae overhead shots showing formations)
   - **Effect:** God's perspective, reveals tactical layout, emphasizes scale of conflict

6. **Worm's Eye View** - Towering creatures, colossal statues, oppressive architecture, ultimate power
   - **Director Reference:** **Guillermo del Toro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Faun towering over Ofelia, labyrinth walls)
   - **Effect:** Camera on ground looking straight up, maximizes sense of scale and power imbalance

## CAMERA MOVEMENT OPTIONS

Movement should enhance storytelling and emotional rhythm. Each movement references specific director techniques from fantasy cinema:

1. **Static (Locked-off)** - *Tripod-mounted, minimal movement*
   - **Director Reference:** **Guillermo del Toro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Pale Man awakening scene - camera perfectly still to build dread)
   - **Use for:** Tension building, ominous moments, formal royal scenes, prophecy revelations
   - **Fantasy Application:** Still frame as magical portal slowly opens, camera unmoving to emphasize the impossibility
   - **Technique:** Stillness creates weight, formality, or dread; subject movement emphasized

2. **Slow Push In (Dolly In)** - *Speed: 0.5-1.5 fps*
   - **Director Reference:** **Peter Jackson** (LOTR: Slow push on The One Ring, Frodo's corrupted face)
   - **Use for:** Building tension, drawing attention to magical artifact, intensifying emotion
   - **Fantasy Application:** Slow dolly toward ancient tome as pages begin to glow
   - **Technique:** Gradual approach intensifies focus, creates hypnotic pull toward subject

3. **Pull Out (Dolly Out)** - *Speed: 0.5-2 fps, revealing scale*
   - **Director Reference:** **Peter Jackson** (LOTR: Pull out from Frodo to reveal Barad-dÃ»r tower, Minas Tirith scale reveal)
   - **Use for:** Revealing hidden scale, showing character's smallness, dramatic revelations
   - **Fantasy Application:** Pull out from character to reveal they're standing in a giant's footprint
   - **Technique:** Reverse dolly reveals context, often shocks with unexpected scale

4. **Tracking/Trucking** - *Lateral movement, Steadicam or dolly*
   - **Director Reference:** **Ang Lee** (Crouching Tiger: Bamboo forest chase, graceful lateral tracking)
   - **Use for:** Following character through magical forest, parallel movement with flying creature
   - **Fantasy Application:** Tracking shot following hero running through crystal cavern, magical reflections passing by
   - **Technique:** Parallel movement maintains consistent framing while showing environmental flow

5. **Crane/Jib** - *Vertical and sweeping movements*
   - **Director Reference:** **Peter Jackson** (LOTR: Iconic crane rise revealing Rivendell, descent into Moria)
   - **Use for:** Epic reveals, rising from character to show vast army, descending into mystical valley
   - **Fantasy Application:** Crane up from intimate campfire scene to reveal dragon circling overhead
   - **Technique:** Vertical movement reveals scale, transitions from intimate to epic or vice versa

6. **Handheld** - *Camera operator shoulder-mount or hand-rig*
   - **Director Reference:** **Guillermo del Toro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Captain Vidal chase scenes, visceral violence)
   - **Use for:** Battle chaos, urgent escape, visceral action, chase sequences
   - **Fantasy Application:** Shaky handheld as character flees through collapsing enchanted ruins
   - **Technique:** Controlled shake creates immediacy, danger, puts audience in the action

7. **Steadicam** - *Stabilized fluid movement*
   - **Director Reference:** **Ang Lee** (Crouching Tiger: Wire-work fight sequences, flowing through courtyards)
   - **Use for:** Graceful magical sequences, flowing through enchanted locations, elegant combat
   - **Fantasy Application:** Smooth Steadicam glide through floating islands connected by light bridges
   - **Technique:** Fluid stabilization creates ethereal quality, suggests magical weightlessness

## LIGHTING OPTIONS

Fantasy lighting should blend natural motivation with magical enhancement. Each option references cinematographer techniques:

1. **God Rays (Volumetric Shafts)** - Sunlight through ancient forest canopy, divine presence, hope breaking through darkness
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Andrew Lesnie** (LOTR: Fangorn Forest, LothlÃ³rien golden light shafts)
   - **Technique:** Volumetric lighting through mist/dust, creates divine/hopeful atmosphere, motivates from sun

2. **Magical Glow (Practical Sources)** - Glowing staffs, enchanted swords, rune circles, bioluminescent plants as motivated light sources
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Guillermo Navarro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Fairy glow, magical book illumination)
   - **Technique:** Practical LED/glow sources in frame, follows inverse square law for realism despite magic

3. **Golden Hour** - Warm, heroic, nostalgic, safe haven moments, victorious endings
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Andrew Lesnie** (LOTR: Shire sequences, Rohan golden plains, victorious endings)
   - **Technique:** 2800K-3500K warm light, shoot during actual golden hour or replicate with gels/color grade

4. **Blue Hour/Twilight** - Mystical transition time, in-between worlds, liminal magic
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Roger Deakins** (Fantasy consultations: Twilight for magical ambiguity)
   - **Technique:** 6000K-8000K cool ambient, shoot 20 minutes after sunset for deep blue sky, adds ethereal quality

5. **Firelight (Warm Amber)** - Campfire gatherings, torch-lit dungeons, dragon breath, cozy tavern safety
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Guillermo Navarro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Captain's fireplace scenes, warm vs cold contrast)
   - **Technique:** 2200K-2700K flickering practical flames, orange glow contrasts with cool shadows

6. **Moonlight (Cool Silver-Blue)** - Night quests, werewolf transformations, elven elegance, mysterious encounters
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Andrew Lesnie** (LOTR: Rivendell night scenes, Minas Tirith moonlight)
   - **Technique:** 7000K-9000K blue-tinted light from above, suggests lunar source, creates mystery

7. **Underlighting** - Ominous villain reveals, magic cauldrons, glowing lava pits, dark rituals
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Guillermo Navarro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Pale Man reveal, horror underlighting)
   - **Technique:** Light from below reverses natural lighting, creates unease/evil connotation

8. **Rim/Edge Lighting** - Heroic silhouettes, separating character from background, divine auras
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Andrew Lesnie** (LOTR: Backlit heroes, separation from dark backgrounds)
   - **Technique:** Strong backlight creates glowing edge around subject, separates from background, suggests divine/heroic

9. **Atmospheric Haze** - Depth layering, mystical fog, ethereal environments, scale enhancement through aerial perspective
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Roger Deakins** (Atmospheric depth techniques: Layered haze for scale)
   - **Technique:** Practical fog/mist creates depth layers, each layer slightly desaturated/lighter for aerial perspective

## COLOR GRADING OPTIONS

Color palette should support emotional tone and fantasy subgenre. Each palette references specific cinematographer work:

1. **Deep Greens & Azures** - Enchanted forests, elven realms, natural magic, pristine wilderness
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Andrew Lesnie** (LOTR: LothlÃ³rien greens/golds, Rivendell azures, Shire vibrant greens)
   - **Technique:** Push greens toward emerald (#2D5016), blues toward azure (#1E90FF), add gold accents (#FFD700), increase saturation +20%

2. **Warm Heroic Glow** - Golden/amber push, victories, safe havens, fellowship moments, sunrise hope
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Andrew Lesnie** (LOTR: Shire warmth, Rohan golden fields, victorious endings)
   - **Technique:** 2800K-3200K color temperature, lift shadows with amber, push highlights golden, warm skin tones

3. **Desaturated Epic** - Muted colors with selective saturation on magical elements, gritty realism with fantasy punctuation
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Roger Deakins** (General technique: Muted reality with punctuated color)
   - **Technique:** Reduce overall saturation -30%, keep magical elements at 100%, creates focus on magic against muted world

4. **Rich Jewel Tones** - Saturated purples, deep reds, royal blues - treasure hoards, magical kingdoms, opulent courts
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Guillermo Navarro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Rich fantasy world colors vs desaturated reality)
   - **Technique:** Increase saturation +40%, push primary colors to jewel tones (ruby red, sapphire blue, amethyst purple)

5. **Cold Steel & Ice** - Blue-cyan palette, winter kingdoms, ice magic, undead encounters
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Andrew Lesnie** (LOTR: Minas Morgul green-blue, Dead Marshes cold palette)
   - **Technique:** 6500K-9000K color temp, push blues/cyans, desaturate warmth, crush blacks for contrast

6. **Fire & Ash** - Orange-red dominance, volcanic lands, dragon lairs, apocalyptic battlefields
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Andrew Lesnie** (LOTR: Mount Doom, Isengard fires, Helm's Deep battle fires)
   - **Technique:** Push oranges/reds in highlights, add ember glow, darken backgrounds, increase contrast between fire and shadow

7. **Ethereal Pastels** - Soft pinks, lavenders, mint greens - fairy realms, dream sequences, spirit worlds
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Guillermo Navarro** (Pan's Labyrinth: Soft fairy realm sequences)
   - **Technique:** Reduce contrast, add pastel tint to highlights, soften overall image, add slight bloom/glow

8. **Monochromatic with Color Splash** - Primarily desaturated with vibrant color only on magical elements (glowing weapons, spell effects)
   - **Cinematographer Reference:** **Roger Deakins** approach (Selective color for emphasis)
   - **Technique:** Desaturate entire image to 20% except specific hue ranges for magic (e.g., only blues/purples stay saturated)

## CINEMATOGRAPHY RULES

**Mandatory Requirements:**

1. **Motivated Lighting:** Even magical light sources must follow natural light physics (inverse square law, realistic shadows, ambient bounce)

2. **Scale Establishment:** Use environmental elements to establish size (human character next to massive tree, tiny figure before colossal gate)

3. **Depth Layering:** Foreground, mid-ground, background elements in every wide shot (mist layers, atmospheric perspective, overlapping mountains)

4. **Practical Integration:** Magical effects should feel grounded through practical light sources, real environmental interaction (glowing staff illuminating character's face, spell light casting realistic shadows)

5. **Movement Motivation:** Camera movement must be motivated by story (push in for tension, pull out for scale reveal, track to follow action) - never arbitrary

6. **Rule of Thirds:** Compose with heroic subjects on power points, horizon lines on upper/lower third, except when breaking for stylistic purpose

7. **Dynamic Symmetry:** Use for temples, throne rooms, formal moments, mystical architecture - creates divine/powerful atmosphere

## TECHNICAL SPECIFICATIONS

**Camera Settings for Fantasy Realism:**
- **Sensor:** Full-frame equivalent for shallow depth and low-light magical environments
- **Aspect Ratio:** 2.39:1 anamorphic (epic scope) or 1.85:1 (more intimate fantasy)
- **Frame Rate:** 24fps (cinematic), 48-60fps for smooth slow-motion combat/magic
- **Shutter Speed:** 1/48s (180Â° shutter) for natural motion blur, 1/96s for sharper action
- **ISO:** Base ISO (400-800) + lighting, push to 3200 for low-light mystical scenes
- **Color Space:** Log profile for maximum grading flexibility (preserve highlight detail in bright magical effects, shadow detail in dark dungeons)

**Lens Characteristics:**
- **Anamorphic:** Horizontal lens flares (from magical light sources), oval bokeh, wide field-of-view for epic vistas
- **Vintage Glass:** Softer contrast, character aberrations, organic feel for ancient world aesthetic
- **Modern Sharp:** Clinical sharpness for contrast with magical elements, modern fantasy, sci-fantasy hybrids

## MOOD & EMOTIONAL TONE GUIDELINES

Match cinematography to fantasy subgenre and emotional beat:

**Epic High Fantasy (LOTR-style):**
- Wide establishing shots with sweeping crane moves
- Golden hour lighting, god rays, hope-through-darkness
- Deep focus to show journey scope
- Warm color grading for fellowship, cool for enemy lands
- Slow, deliberate camera movement showing scale

**Dark Fantasy (Pan's Labyrinth-style):**
- Contrast between cold reality (desaturated blue) and warm fantasy (amber/gold)
- Low-key lighting, rich shadows, practical sources
- Dutch angles for unsettling moments
- Close-ups to emphasize horror/wonder
- Handheld for visceral fear, smooth for magical beauty

**Sword & Sorcery (Conan-style):**
- Gritty desaturated palette with selective color on blood/magic
- Handheld combat, Steadicam for hero prowling
- Low angles for powerful warrior framing
- Harsh sunlight or firelight, minimal magical glow
- Medium shots for visceral action clarity

**Romantic Fantasy:**
- Soft ethereal lighting, pastel color grading
- Gentle camera movements (slow dolly, graceful crane)
- Close-ups and medium close-ups for emotional connection
- Golden hour heavily featured
- Shallow depth of field for dreamy separation

**Urban Fantasy:**
- Modern city lighting (neon, streetlights) mixed with magical glows
- Handheld for gritty realism, Steadicam for magical sequences
- Desaturated urban backgrounds, saturated magical elements
- Eye-level angles for relatability
- Quick cuts between mundane and magical

- Keep magical effects using same color logic (healing = warm gold, destructive = cold blue, etc.)
- Use recurring visual motifs (god rays for hope, shadows for danger)

**Scale and Wonder:**
- Fantasy should evoke awe - use scale contrast, atmospheric depth, epic scope
- Every wide shot should contain at least 3 depth layers (foreground/mid/background)
- Magical elements should feel integrated, not pasted on - interact with environment lighting

**Grounded Fantasy:**
- Even in most fantastical scenes, maintain some realistic anchor (real physics on cloth, natural lighting behavior, genuine human emotion)
- Avoid cartoon physics unless intentionally stylized
- Practical effects thinking: "How would we light this if the magic was a real light source?"
